What makes a good website?

A good website is subjective, but a functional website is easier to define objectively. A bad website is a site that doesn't give the user a way to easily to navigate and read (and/or see) through its content. Not using annoyingly sized or colored type and images certainly helps make a website more appealing, too.
Here are a couple examples of a "good" website and "bad" website based on personal preference.




The Bad: Time Cube

Time Cube is a site legendary for it's befuddling awfulness. For the most part, all of its content consists of a single very long, very disjointed rant. What takes Time Cube from strange conspiracy theory expose to a completely insane and somewhat disturbing experience is how the site is designed. Huge type dominates, sometimes black, red, or blue, sometimes bolded, sometimes italicized, never following any predictable formatting rules. The pages -- laid out sequentially as if pages in a book and not on a website -- are all incredibly long due to the giant, rambling text, making it tiresome to scroll to the bottom, much less read the content itself. Time Cube shows how bad design can make the curiously crazy into flat out terrifying insanity.




The Good: Gmail

Gmail is a very well known and popular email service, and may not seem like anything special on the surface as far as design goes, but that's what makes it successful. Gmail's interface is designed to be comfortable and functional. It doesn't cover your inbox with flashy images or script that takes an eternity to load. It's layout is basic, readable, easy to navigate, and most importantly, accessible. Anyone can read through their Gmail inbox without getting a headache from garish font sizes and colors or overly intricate design. It's professionally minimalist and it works.
